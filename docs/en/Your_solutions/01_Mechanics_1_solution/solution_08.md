# 8. Circular Motion: Centripetal Acceleration at the Equator

This analysis calculates the acceleration experienced by an object due to the Earth's rotation at the equator.

---

## 1. Key Definitions and Formulas

To solve for centripetal acceleration on a rotating sphere, we use the following concepts:

### Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
The acceleration directed toward the center of a circular path that keeps an object moving in a circle.

$$
a_c = \frac{v^2}{R} = \omega^2 R
$$

### Angular Velocity ($\omega$)
The rate at which an object rotates, measured in radians per second. For a full rotation ($2\pi$ radians) over a period ($T$):

$$
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}
$$

### Linear Velocity ($v$)
The speed of an object moving along the circumference:

$$
v = \frac{2\pi R}{T}
$$

---

## 2. Problem Statement

Calculate the centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) of a person standing on the Earth's equator.

**Given Data:**
* **Earth's Radius ($R$):** $6378\text{ km} = 6,378,000\text{ m}$
* **Rotation Period ($T$):** Approximately $24\text{ hours}$ (1 sidereal day is more precise, but $24\text{h}$ is standard for this problem).

---

## 3. Step-by-Step Solution

### Step 1: Convert Units to SI (meters and seconds)
Physics calculations are most reliable when using the International System of Units.

* **Radius ($R$):**

$$
R = 6378 \times 10^3 = 6,378,000\text{ m}
$$

* **Time ($T$):**

$$
T = 24 \times 60 \times 60 = 86,400\text{ s}
$$



### Step 2: Calculate Angular Velocity ($\omega$)
We find how many radians the Earth rotates per second:

$$
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{86,400} \approx 7.272 \times 10^{-5}\text{ rad/s}
$$

### Step 3: Calculate Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
Using the formula $a_c = \omega^2 R$:

$$
a_c = (7.272 \times 10^{-5})^2 \times 6,378,000
$$

$$
a_c = (5.288 \times 10^{-9}) \times 6,378,000
$$

$$
a_c \approx 0.0337\text{ m/s}^2
$$

---

## 4. Final Result and Interpretation

The centripetal acceleration at the equator is:

$$
a_c \approx 0.0337\text{ m/s}^2
$$

### Interpretation
1. **Comparison to Gravity:** Earth's gravitational acceleration ($g$) is $\approx 9.81\text{ m/s}^2$. The centripetal acceleration is only about **0.34%** of gravity.
2. **Effect on Weight:** Because this acceleration points toward the center of the Earth, it "uses up" a tiny bit of the gravitational pull. As a result, you actually weigh slightly less at the equator than at the poles!
