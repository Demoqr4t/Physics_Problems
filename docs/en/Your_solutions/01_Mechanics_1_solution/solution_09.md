# 9. Momentum Comparison: Fly vs. Tennis Ball

This document analyzes and compares the linear momentum of two objects with different masses and velocities.

---

## 1. Key Definitions and Formulas

### Linear Momentum ($p$)
Momentum is a vector quantity that represents the "quantity of motion" an object has. It depends on how much stuff is moving (mass) and how fast it's moving (velocity).

$$
p = m \cdot v
$$

**Where:**
* **$p$**: Momentum (measured in $\text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s}$)
* **$m$**: Mass (must be in **kilograms**, $\text{kg}$)
* **$v$**: Velocity (measured in $\text{m/s}$)



### Unit Conversion
Since standard units for momentum use kilograms, we must convert grams to kilograms:

$$
1 \text{ gram} = 0.001 \text{ kg}
$$

---

## 2. Problem Statement

Which object has greater momentum?
1.  **A Fly:** mass = $2\text{ g}$, velocity = $10\text{ m/s}$
2.  **A Tennis Ball:** mass = $60\text{ g}$, velocity = $1\text{ m/s}$

---

## 3. Step-by-Step Solution

### Step 1: Convert Masses to Kilograms
To get an accurate comparison in SI units, we convert both masses first.

* **Fly mass ($m_1$):**
$$
2\text{ g} = 2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ kg} = 0.002 \text{ kg}
$$

* **Tennis Ball mass ($m_2$):**
$$
60\text{ g} = 60 \times 10^{-3} \text{ kg} = 0.060 \text{ kg}
$$

---

### Step 2: Calculate Momentum for the Fly ($p_{fly}$)
Using the formula $p = mv$:

$$
p_{fly} = 0.002 \text{ kg} \times 10 \text{ m/s}
$$

$$
p_{fly} = 0.02 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m/s}
$$

---

### Step 3: Calculate Momentum for the Tennis Ball ($p_{ball}$)
Using the formula $p = mv$:

$$
p_{ball} = 0.060 \text{ kg} \times 1 \text{ m/s}
$$

$$
p_{ball} = 0.06 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m/s}
$$

---

## 4. Final Comparison and Conclusion

Comparing the two results:
* **Fly:** $0.02 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m/s}$
* **Tennis Ball:** $0.06 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m/s}$

Since $0.06 > 0.02$, the **tennis ball** has a greater momentum.

> **Note:** Even though the fly is moving $10$ times faster than the tennis ball, the tennis ball's mass is $30$ times greater than the fly's mass. In the "tug-of-war" between mass and velocity, the tennis ball's mass wins this round.
